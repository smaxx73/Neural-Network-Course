\section{Why Squaring?}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
Without squaring, positive and negative errors cancel each other out — a model
that over-predicts by 5 and under-predicts by 5 would appear to have zero error.
Squaring makes all contributions positive and penalises large errors more heavily
(quadratic growth), which drives the optimiser to reduce the worst predictions first.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Derivative of a Loss}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
Let $u = 4 - 2w$, so $L = u^2$. By the chain rule:
$$\frac{dL}{dw} = 2u \cdot \frac{du}{dw} = 2(4 - 2w)(-2)$$
\begin{tcolorbox}[answerbox]
$$\frac{dL}{dw} = -4(4 - 2w) = 8w - 16$$
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{tcolorbox}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Gradient Direction}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
We should \textbf{increase} $w$. The gradient descent update rule is:
$$w \leftarrow w - \eta\,\frac{dL}{dw}$$
A negative gradient means subtracting a negative number, i.e.\ $w$ increases.
Intuitively, the loss curve slopes downward to the right at $w=0$, so moving
right (increasing $w$) reduces the loss.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Loss Landscape}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
A \textbf{local minimum} is a point where the loss is lower than all nearby points,
but not necessarily the lowest overall. A \textbf{global minimum} is the single
lowest point on the entire loss surface.

For \textbf{linear models with MSE loss}, the loss surface is a convex paraboloid
(a smooth bowl), so there is exactly one minimum, which is both local and global.
Non-linear models such as MLPs can have multiple local minima and saddle points.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Partial Derivatives}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
Let $u = 2 - w_1 - 3w_2$, so $L = u^2$.

\begin{align*}
\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1} &= 2u \cdot (-1) = -2(2 - w_1 - 3w_2) \\[0.4em]
\frac{\partial L}{\partial w_2} &= 2u \cdot (-3) = -6(2 - w_1 - 3w_2)
\end{align*}

At $(w_1, w_2) = (1, 0)$: $u = 2 - 1 - 0 = 1$.

\begin{tcolorbox}[answerbox]
$$\nabla L\big|_{(1,\,0)} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -6 \end{bmatrix}$$
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Diverging Loss}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
The most likely cause is a \textbf{learning rate that is too large}. With an
oversized $\eta$, each update overshoots the minimum — the weights jump past it
and land on the other side at a higher loss value, which compounds with each step.

\textbf{Fix:} reduce the learning rate (e.g.\ divide by 10) and restart training.
A useful diagnostic is to plot the loss curve on a log scale; divergence appears
as a rapid upward trend rather than a plateau.
\end{tcolorbox}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Bridge to Neural Networks}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
The four-step recipe (forward pass $\to$ loss $\to$ gradients $\to$ weight update)
is identical. The key difference is \textbf{step 3 — computing gradients for hidden
layers}. In linear regression the gradient is a single, direct derivative. In an
MLP, weights in early layers affect the loss \emph{indirectly}, through the hidden
activations:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W^{(1)}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h} \cdot \frac{\partial h}{\partial W^{(1)}}$$

This requires the \textbf{chain rule} applied layer by layer, which is exactly what
\textbf{backpropagation} (Session~6) implements efficiently.
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Chain Rule — Symbolic Derivative}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
Let $z = 3x - 2$, so $y = \sigma(z)$. By the chain rule:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{d\sigma}{dz} \cdot \frac{dz}{dx} = \sigma(z)\bigl(1 - \sigma(z)\bigr) \cdot 3$$

\begin{tcolorbox}[answerbox]
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3\,\sigma(3x-2)\bigl(1 - \sigma(3x-2)\bigr)$$
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{tcolorbox}

% ------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Chain Rule — Numerical}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
Let $u = 2x + 1$, so $y = u^3$. By the chain rule:
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3u^2 \cdot \frac{du}{dx} = 3(2x+1)^2 \cdot 2 = 6(2x+1)^2$$

At $x = 1$: $u = 3$, so:

\begin{tcolorbox}[answerbox]
$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{x=1} = 6 \times 3^2 = 54$$
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Backward Pass by Hand}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
Using the values from Question~\ref{sec:forward-pass-by-hand}:
$e \approx -0.2891$, $\hat{y} \approx 0.7109$, $z = 0.9$, $x = 1.5$.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
Step & Gradient & Value \\
\midrule
1 & $\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial e} = 2e$
  & $2 \times (-0.2891) \approx -0.5782$ \\[1.2em]
2 & $\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} = \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial e} \cdot 1$
  & $\approx -0.5782$ \\[1.2em]
3 & $\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial z} = \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z))$
  & $-0.5782 \times 0.7109 \times 0.2891 \approx -0.1189$ \\[1.2em]
4 & $\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w} = \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial z} \cdot x$
  & $-0.1189 \times 1.5 \approx -0.1784$ \\[1.2em]
5 & $\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial b} = \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial z} \cdot 1$
  & $\approx -0.1189$ \\[1.2em]
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Both gradients are \textbf{negative}: gradient descent will \emph{increase} $w$ and $b$,
pushing $\hat{y}$ closer to $1$. $\checkmark$

\medskip
\textbf{Weight update} with $\eta = 1.0$:
\begin{align*}
w &\leftarrow 0.8 - 1.0 \times (-0.1784) = 0.9784 \\
b &\leftarrow -0.3 - 1.0 \times (-0.1189) = -0.1811
\end{align*}

\begin{tcolorbox}[answerbox]
$w_{\text{new}} \approx 0.9784$,\quad $b_{\text{new}} \approx -0.1811$
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Error Signal $\delta$}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
Given $\delta^{(2)} = -0.20$,\;
$W^{(2)} = [0.6,\; {-0.4}]^T$,\;
$z^{(1)} = [0.6,\; {-0.3}]^T$.

\medskip
\textbf{(a)} Pull the error back through the weights:
\[
  (W^{(2)})^T\,\delta^{(2)}
  = \begin{bmatrix} 0.6 \\ -0.4 \end{bmatrix} \times (-0.20)
  = \begin{bmatrix} -0.120 \\ 0.080 \end{bmatrix}
\]

\medskip
\textbf{(b)} Sigmoid derivative at each hidden pre-activation:
\begin{align*}
\sigma(0.6)  &\approx 0.6457, & \sigma'(0.6)  &= 0.6457 \times 0.3543 \approx 0.2288 \\
\sigma(-0.3) &\approx 0.4256, & \sigma'(-0.3) &= 0.4256 \times 0.5744 \approx 0.2445
\end{align*}
\[
  \sigma'(z^{(1)}) \approx \begin{bmatrix} 0.2288 \\ 0.2445 \end{bmatrix}
\]

\medskip
\textbf{(c)} Element-wise product:
\[
  \delta^{(1)}
  = \begin{bmatrix} -0.120 \\ 0.080 \end{bmatrix}
    \odot
    \begin{bmatrix} 0.2288 \\ 0.2445 \end{bmatrix}
\]

\begin{tcolorbox}[answerbox]
$$\delta^{(1)} \approx \begin{bmatrix} -0.0275 \\ 0.0196 \end{bmatrix}$$
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{tcolorbox}

\section{Vanishing Gradients — Conceptual}

\begin{tcolorbox}[solutionbox]
\textbf{(a)} At each layer the backward-propagating gradient is multiplied by
$\sigma'(z) \leq 0.25$. After $L$ sigmoid layers the gradient is scaled by a
factor of at most $0.25^L$, which shrinks \emph{exponentially} toward zero.
Early-layer weights therefore receive gradients so small that they effectively
stop learning — this is the \textbf{vanishing gradient problem}.

\medskip
\textbf{(b)} The \textbf{Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU)} was introduced to address this:
\[
  \operatorname{ReLU}(z) = \max(0,\, z),
  \qquad
  \operatorname{ReLU}'(z) =
  \begin{cases} 1 & z > 0 \\ 0 & z \leq 0 \end{cases}
\]
When a neuron is active ($z > 0$), the derivative is $1$, so the gradient passes
through unchanged and does not shrink as it flows backward through ReLU layers.
\end{tcolorbox}
